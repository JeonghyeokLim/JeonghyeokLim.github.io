Caster-Aware Control for Industrial AMRs

Jeonghyeok Lim · Multi-Stage Caster-aware Control (MSC)

Overview

Industrial autonomous mobile robots commonly use passive caster wheels because they provide high load capacity with a simple mechanical structure. During low-speed turns and high-curvature maneuvers, however, caster misalignment produces nonlinear contact forces and a substantial gap between the commanded body velocity and the motion realized by the robot.

Multi-Stage Caster-aware Control (MSC) addresses this planning–execution gap through a hierarchical framework that combines uncertainty-aware nonlinear model predictive control with a recurrent, reinforcement-learning-based execution layer. Rather than treating caster compensation as a separate post-processing step, MSC coordinates high-level motion planning, latent caster-state inference, and wheel-level execution within one control pipeline.

Control Architecture

1. CU-MPC Planning

The high-level controller tracks the reference path while accounting for position, body velocity, angular velocity, caster state, and uncertainty. Predictive uncertainty is used to regulate caster-aware costs and avoid overly aggressive commands in sensitive transition regimes.

2. Recurrent State Inference

A GRU encoder processes an observation history and forms a latent representation of the partially observed caster dynamics. A decoder estimates the current caster configuration and predicts its near-term distribution.

3. RL-Based Execution

The actor transforms the high-level linear and angular velocity references into wheel-velocity targets adapted to the inferred contact condition. A wheel-level PI controller then applies the corresponding torque commands to the simulated or physical AMR.

Training and Deployment

During training, privileged caster information supervises the latent estimator and supports critic learning. At deployment, the policy operates from measurable observations and their temporal history, allowing caster-related states to be inferred under partial observability without requiring the privileged simulation signals.

The key design idea is to let uncertainty influence both planning and execution: CU-MPC moderates high-level commands when caster predictions are unreliable, while the low-level policy adapts wheel commands to the estimated caster condition.

Validation

The framework is evaluated in simulation and on a caster-equipped AMR. The experiments focus on caster-sensitive motions such as tight turns and transition maneuvers, examining path-tracking accuracy, caster-state estimation, caster misalignment, and robustness of the realized wheel-level execution.
